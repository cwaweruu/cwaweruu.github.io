Empowering C-Suite Decisions with HR Analytics

The Problem

A company's HR function held data on 290 employees across headcount, sick leave, pay distribution, tenure, and demographics — but it all sat in spreadsheets. Senior leadership made headcount and compensation decisions without trend data or a shared baseline. The goal was to build a dashboard that transforms HR data into a strategic management tool.

The Approach

I analysed the workforce dataset in Power BI, building calculated columns and DAX measures for four categories: headcount by department and tenure band; sick leave rates over time; pay distribution by gender and role level; and hiring trend analysis by quarter.

The report was structured into three pages — Overview, Workforce Demographics, and Pay & Leave — each designed for a different use case. The Overview was built for board-level summaries; the Demographics and Pay pages for HR review and compliance checks, with drill-through capability. Every measure was documented in plain English so the HR team could own and maintain the report independently.

Key Findings

Sick leave rates were substantially higher in two departments compared to the company baseline — pointing to workload or management issues requiring immediate investigation.

Pay distribution analysis revealed a measurable gap in median pay between gender groups at mid-level roles — invisible in the raw spreadsheet data without visualisation.

Hiring had accelerated sharply in two consecutive quarters but was concentrated in one department, creating an organisational capacity imbalance with downstream implications.

Impact

The dashboard gave HR and C-suite leadership a tool they could update monthly and use directly in board briefings. The pay gap finding required immediate follow-up. The project also established a consistent data model and shared metric definitions the HR team could use as a baseline going forward.

What I Learned

HR data is sensitive and the dashboard design choices matter enormously. I had to think carefully about how to present the pay gap finding — as a factual observation framed with statistical context, not as a judgement. I also learned that when cohorts are small (some departments had under 20 people), you must be explicit about the limits of what the data can support. Visualising small-sample differences as bar charts without confidence intervals is misleading even when the intent is honest.
