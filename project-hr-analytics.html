Empowering C-Suite Decisions with HR Analytics

The Problem

A company's HR function held data on 290 employees across headcount, sick leave, pay distribution, tenure, and demographics — but that data sat in spreadsheets accessible to only a few people and offering no historical view. Senior leadership made headcount and compensation decisions without trend data, pattern recognition, or a shared baseline. The goal was to build a dashboard that transforms HR data into a strategic management tool — one that would remain useful beyond its first use.

The Approach

I analysed the workforce dataset in Power BI, building calculated columns and DAX measures to produce four categories of insight: headcount by department and tenure band; sick leave rates over time; pay distribution by gender and role level; and hiring trend analysis by quarter.

The report was structured into three pages — Overview, Workforce Demographics, and Pay & Leave — each designed for a different use case. The Overview page was built for board-level summaries: quick, visual, one page. The Demographics and Pay pages were built for HR review and compliance checks, with drill-through capability so users could investigate patterns without leaving the report.

Every measure was documented with a plain-English description so the HR team could own and maintain the report independently.

Key Findings

Sick leave rates were substantially higher in two departments compared to the company baseline — pointing to either workload problems or management issues in those specific teams that required immediate investigation.

Pay distribution analysis revealed a measurable gap in median pay between gender groups at mid-level roles — a finding that would have remained invisible in the raw spreadsheet data without visualisation.

Hiring had accelerated sharply in two consecutive quarters but was concentrated in one department, creating an organisational capacity imbalance with downstream implications for training, management spans, and culture.

Impact

The dashboard gave HR and C-suite leadership a tool they could update monthly and use directly in board briefings. The pay gap finding in particular required immediate follow-up — it would have remained invisible in the raw spreadsheet data. Beyond the specific findings, the project also established a consistent data model and shared metric definitions that the HR team could use going forward.

What I Learned

"HR data is sensitive and the dashboard design choices matter enormously. I had to think carefully about how to present the pay gap finding — as a factual observation framed with statistical context, not as a judgement or headline. I also learned that when your cohorts are small (some departments had under 20 people), you need to be explicit about the limits of what the data can support. Visualising small-sample differences as bar charts without confidence intervals is misleading even when the intent is honest."
