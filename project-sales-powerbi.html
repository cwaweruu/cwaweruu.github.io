Regional Revenue & Market Performance Dashboard

The Problem

A business operating across multiple regions had no consolidated view of revenue performance, market penetration, or growth KPIs. Regional managers worked from separate reports built on different assumptions, with no common baseline and no way to compare performance side-by-side. As a result, resource allocation decisions were driven by seniority and advocacy — not evidence. The goal was a single executive dashboard that enabled cross-regional comparison and identified where investment would have the highest impact.

The Approach

I consolidated multi-regional sales data in Power BI, which first required standardising currency, date formats, and regional naming conventions across the source files. These alignment decisions — what counts as "Region A," what exchange rate to use, how to treat partial months — were documented explicitly so the business understood what assumptions underpinned the numbers.

I built DAX measures for: revenue by region; period-over-period growth rates; market penetration as a percentage of addressable market; and ROI by marketing channel. The final dashboard used a consistent layout across all regions so that comparison was visually immediate rather than requiring cognitive work to interpret.

Key Findings

Two regions were consistently underperforming relative to their addressable market size — suggesting an execution or go-to-market problem, not a market problem. The gap was not visible before the dashboard because those regions were measured only against their own prior periods.

Marketing ROI varied significantly by channel: the highest-spend channel was not the highest-return channel. The visualised gap was the single insight that changed the quarterly budget allocation.

Revenue concentration was high: two regions contributed a disproportionate share of total revenue — creating organisational risk that was invisible in the regional-only view each manager had previously used.

Impact

The dashboard directly informed a resource reallocation discussion at the executive level. The ROI gap between marketing channels was immediately actionable: the next quarter's budget was adjusted before the dashboard had been live for two weeks. Beyond the immediate budget change, the project established a shared data model and metric definitions that the team could use as a baseline going forward.

What I Learned

"The hardest part of a regional dashboard is not the DAX — it's convincing regional stakeholders that a common metric definition is more valuable than their local customisations. Every region had a reason why their numbers should be measured differently. But a dashboard that can be interpreted differently by each user is not a dashboard; it's a set of separate reports with shared colours. Alignment on definitions precedes alignment on strategy."
