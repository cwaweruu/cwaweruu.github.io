Reducing Enrollment Drop-Off: A/B Testing at Udacity

The Problem

Udacity wanted to test a proposed product change: displaying a time-commitment warning to prospective students before they enrolled in a free trial. The hypothesis was that this filter would reduce early drop-offs — surfacing students unlikely to complete the course — without harming the number of students who ultimately pay. The question was whether to launch.

The Approach

I designed the experiment with properly specified metrics before looking at any results — the single most important discipline in A/B testing. The metrics were: gross conversion (did the change reduce free trial sign-ups?), retention (of those who signed up, did completion rates improve?), and net conversion (did the proportion of users who ultimately paid hold steady or improve?).

I determined the required sample size using statistical power calculations at 80% power and 5% significance, ran two-tailed hypothesis tests with 95% confidence intervals, and checked for practical significance in addition to statistical significance — because a result can be statistically real but too small to justify the operational cost of launching.

All analysis was conducted in R.

Key Findings

Gross conversion decreased significantly in the test group — the time-commitment warning did filter out students unlikely to complete the trial, as hypothesised.

Net conversion showed no statistically significant improvement — the students who remained after seeing the warning did not pay at a meaningfully higher rate than the control group.

The retention metric could not be reliably evaluated within the experiment timeline — the required sample size to achieve adequate statistical power was impractically large given the experiment window.

Impact

The recommendation was do not launch in the current form. The change reduced enrollments without a corresponding improvement in payments — a net negative business outcome. The warning filtered out potential students without converting the remaining ones at a higher rate.

This is a case where rigorous analysis prevented a costly mistake rather than enabling a win. The no-launch recommendation was grounded in the data rather than intuition, and the write-up documented exactly which conditions would need to be true for a future version of the experiment to be worth running.

What I Learned

"A/B testing is as much about knowing what not to build as what to build. The discipline of specifying primary and secondary metrics — and their success thresholds — before seeing any results is what keeps analysis honest. If you define success after the fact, you can always find it. The Udacity experiment was valuable precisely because it returned a clear no."
