Reducing Enrollment Drop-Off: A/B Testing at Udacity

The Problem

Udacity wanted to test a proposed product change: displaying a time-commitment warning to prospective students before they enrolled in a free trial. The hypothesis was that this filter would reduce early drop-offs without harming the number of students who ultimately pay. The question was whether to launch.

The Approach

I designed the experiment with properly specified metrics before looking at any results — the single most important discipline in A/B testing. The metrics were: gross conversion (did the change reduce free trial sign-ups?), retention (of those who signed up, did completion rates improve?), and net conversion (did the proportion who ultimately paid hold steady or improve?).

I determined the required sample size using statistical power calculations at 80% power and 5% significance, ran two-tailed hypothesis tests with 95% confidence intervals, and checked for practical significance as well as statistical significance — because a result can be statistically real but too small to justify the cost of launching.

Key Findings

Gross conversion decreased significantly in the test group — the warning did filter out students unlikely to complete, as hypothesised.

Net conversion showed no statistically significant improvement — the students who remained after seeing the warning did not pay at a meaningfully higher rate than the control group.

The retention metric could not be reliably evaluated within the experiment window — the required sample size to achieve adequate statistical power was impractically large.

Impact

The recommendation was do not launch in the current form. The change reduced enrollments without a corresponding improvement in payments — a net negative business outcome. This is a case where rigorous analysis prevented a costly mistake rather than enabling a win.

What I Learned

A/B testing is as much about knowing what not to build as what to build. The discipline of specifying primary and secondary metrics — and their success thresholds — before seeing any results is what keeps analysis honest. If you define success after the fact, you can always find it. The Udacity experiment was valuable precisely because it returned a clear no.
