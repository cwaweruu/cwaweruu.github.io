Forecasting Employment Risk Under EU Green Transition Policy

The Problem

The European Economic and Social Committee (EESC) needed an evidence base for assessing the social cost of inaction on the EU's Just Transition Directive (JTD). The central question was not whether the green transition would cause disruption — it would — but rather: what is the comparative cost of acting weakly versus acting decisively? And which Member States, sectors, and population groups bear the heaviest burden under each scenario?

Without this modelling, EU policymakers were being asked to vote on implementation design without a quantified understanding of the trade-offs between ambition and social risk.

The Approach

Working at Visionary Analytics as part of an EU-commissioned research team, I contributed to econometric and scenario modelling work that projected outcomes to 2045 across seven EU Member States: Germany, France, Poland, Spain, Italy, Hungary, and Romania.

The model used a partial-equilibrium framework with three scenarios: Business As Usual (BAU), capturing current policy trajectories; Weak JTD implementation, where the Directive is enacted but enforcement and investment are below target; and Strong JTD implementation, representing full policy ambition with adequate funding and coordination.

My contributions included building scenario comparison tables in Python, visualising employment and poverty outcomes by Member State for policymaker audiences, and contributing to the written analysis of regional disparity effects. The outputs had to be simultaneously rigorous enough to survive academic scrutiny and clear enough to be useful in a policy briefing.

Key Findings

Under the BAU scenario, projected employment losses in high-carbon industries exceed green sector job gains in four of seven Member States by 2031 — meaning the transition, without active policy, produces net job losses in the near term.

Strong JTD implementation significantly reduces net employment disruption by 2040 compared to weak implementation — but requires front-loaded investment commitments that weak implementation cannot sustain, widening the divergence over time.

Regional inequality effects are highly heterogeneous: the same national policy produces dramatically different outcomes in high-carbon regions versus service-economy regions, meaning national averages systematically obscure the groups most at risk.

Impact

The report was published by the EESC in January 2026 and submitted to EU policymakers as part of the evidence base for Just Transition Directive implementation design. It is publicly available, citable, and bears the EESC institutional imprimatur — making it part of the formal EU policy record.

For the research team, it demonstrated that a robust multi-scenario quantitative model could be delivered within the constraints of an EU research tender — a proof of concept for future evidence-based policy work of this kind.

What I Learned

"Policy modelling is fundamentally about communicating uncertainty honestly. Every scenario projection carries assumptions that may not hold — the analyst's job is to make those assumptions visible and explicit, not to hide them inside a single headline number. The three-scenario structure exists precisely to resist the temptation of false precision. A single projection says: this is what will happen. Three scenarios say: this is the range of what is likely, and here is what determines which end of the range you land on. That second framing is far more useful to a policymaker."
