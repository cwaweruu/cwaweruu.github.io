Weekly Retention Cohort Analysis for a Subscription Platform

The Problem

A subscription-based business was monitoring retention with traditional monthly metrics. The problem: monthly aggregates were masking short-term churn patterns that, by the time they appeared in reports, were already too late to address. If a cohort starts falling apart in week one, you will not see it in your monthly numbers until next month — when most of those subscribers are already gone.

The Approach

I extracted data from turing_data_analytics.subscriptions, grouping users by their subscription start week — their "cohort" — and tracked how many remained active at weeks 1 through 6. The cohort table was built in SQL and retention rates were calculated as a percentage of each cohort's starting size. This normalisation is critical: it allows direct comparison across cohorts regardless of absolute size differences.

The final output was a retention matrix — cohorts as rows, weeks as columns, retention rate as each cell — designed to be read visually and acted on operationally.

Key Findings

The sharpest drop occurred between Week 0 and Week 1 across nearly all cohorts — indicating an onboarding or early-value problem, not a long-term engagement problem.

Retention stabilised from Week 3 onwards, suggesting subscribers who survived the first two weeks had substantially higher long-term value — a distinct, high-priority segment.

One cohort showed an anomalously high Week 2 drop that could not be explained by seasonal patterns — pointing to a specific product or external event worth independent investigation.

Impact

The analysis shifted the business's intervention strategy from reactive (monthly churn reports) to proactive (targeting Week 0–1 with improved onboarding). The actionable window for retention is during the first two weeks — not after month-end reports arrive.

What I Learned

Cohort analysis is only as useful as the cohort definition. I chose subscription start week, but the cohort could equally be defined by first purchase, first login, or first meaningful in-product action. The choice of cohort definition is the most consequential analytical decision in the entire project. It determines what story you can and cannot tell.
